## Introduction

Every year, millions of Christians around the world celebrate the Easter Triduum: three days commemorating the passion, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ. But beyond its religious significance, these three days contain a universal archetype: the cycle of death and rebirth that underlies all profound transformation.

From the perspective of the I Ching - the ancient Chinese Book of Changes - this cycle finds a surprising parallel in three specific hexagrams: **[23 (Bō, Disintegration)]({{% relref "/hexagramas/hex23" %}})**, **[2 (Kūn, The Earth)]({{% relref "/hexagramas/hex02" %}})** , and **[24 (Fù, The Return)]({{% relref "/hexagramas/hex24" %}})** . The structure of these hexagrams, their arrangement in the King Wen sequence, and their internal relationships reveal an almost mathematical codification of the Paschal mystery.

In this article, we will explore, step by step, how the I Ching offers us a **notational tool** for thinking about death and resurrection, and why Holy Saturday - that seemingly empty day between Friday and Sunday - is actually the silent heart of the entire process.

---

## Good Friday: Hexagram 23 - ䷖ - Bō (剝), Disintegration

> *Upper:* ☶ Gèn, The Mountain  
> *Lower:* ☷ Kūn, The Earth

[Hexagram 23]({{% relref "/hexagramas/hex23" %}}), known as *Bō* (meaning "to wear away," "to strip," or "to disintegrate"), describes the process in which structures break down from their foundation. It is a time when what was once solid crumbles, and what is below advances to overthrow what is above[^1].

Its structure is brutally eloquent: **five yin lines (broken) at the base, forcefully undermining until they collapse the single yang line (unbroken) at the top**.

In I Ching terms, yin lines represent the receptive, the dark, that which yields; yang lines represent the active, the luminous, that which sustains. Five yin lines collapsing a single yang line is the image of total collapse: the end of the old, the death of what once gave life.

The upper trigram of 23 is **Gèn (艮), The Mountain**. And what is Golgotha if not a mountain? That yang line in the highest position, surrounded and destroyed by the yin rising from below, is the image of Christ on the cross: the Righteous One overthrown by injustice, Life delivered to death.

> *...This is not a matter of human action, but of temporal conditions which, according to celestial laws, also show a change of increase and decrease, fullness and emptiness. These temporal conditions cannot be resisted.*

But hexagram 23 is not just destruction. It teaches that **everything accumulated to excess eventually cracks, and that disintegration opens the way for something new**. What dies transforms into seed for what is to come. The wise person, the text says, withdraws inward, preserves what is essential, and awaits the rebirth that follows the fall.

That "rebirth that follows" is precisely what happens in the days that follow.

## Holy Saturday: Hexagram 2 - ䷁ - Kūn (坤), The Earth

> *Upper:* ☷ Kūn, The Earth  
> *Lower:* ☷ Kūn, The Earth

Holy Saturday is the day of the tomb. There is no movement. No action. It is a day of pure stillness, of silence, of waiting.

In the I Ching, that total stillness corresponds to **[hexagram 2, Kūn (坤), The Earth]({{% relref "/hexagramas/hex02" %}})** . It consists exclusively of six yin (broken) lines, without a single line of yang energy. It is absolute receptivity, the fertile void, the matrix of all possibility.

Kūn is the perfect complement to hexagram 1 (Qián, The Creative). They are not opposites that fight, but complements that need each other. If Heaven (Qián) is the engendering force, Earth (Kūn) is what nourishes and sustains. It is the feminine energy of the universe: open, fertile, ready to receive.

> *The quality of the Earth is receptive devotion. The Noble One, broad in nature, sustains the outer world.*

Holy Saturday is that state of pure receptivity. It is the earth receiving the dead body. It is the silence that precedes the word. It is the non-time where the old completely disintegrates so that the new can germinate.

## Easter Sunday: Hexagram 24 - ䷗ - Fù (復), The Return

> *Upper:* ☷ Kūn, The Earth  
> *Lower:* ☳ Zhèn, The Thunder

Easter Sunday is the resurrection. In the I Ching, this is **[hexagram 24, Fù (復), The Return]({{% relref "/hexagramas/hex24" %}})** .

Its structure is the inverse of 23: now we have **one yang line at the bottom and five yin lines above**. It is the thunder under the earth, awakening and pushing upward. It is the moment when yang energy returns from the deepest point to initiate new life.

The ideogram *fù* translates as "to return, to come back, to revert." But it is not a simple return to the starting point: it is a **transformed return**, a coming back that carries with it the experience of death and the promise of new life.

> *The time of change is indicated because, after the dark lines have pushed all the light lines upward, now a light line enters again from below into the sign. The time of darkness has passed. The solstice brings the victory of light. The sign corresponds to the eleventh month, the month of the winter solstice (December-January).*

Just as spring is not a mere return of winter but an irruption of new life, the resurrection is not a simple reanimation: it is the definitive overcoming of death. It is the yang returning from below, not to restore the old, but to inaugurate the new.

## The Paradox of the King Wen Sequence

So far, the correspondence is clear:

- **Good Friday → Hexagram 23 (Disintegration)**
- **Holy Saturday → Hexagram 2 (The Earth)**
- **Easter Sunday → Hexagram 24 (The Return)**

But there is a problem. If we look at the **King Wen sequence** - the classical ordering of the 64 hexagrams - we see that **23 is directly followed by 24**. There is no intermediate hexagram for Holy Saturday in that sequence.

Why?

The answer is key to understanding the profound meaning of Holy Saturday.

In the I Ching, the lines of a hexagram (numbered from bottom to top, 1 to 6) represent a **temporal progression** within a given situation:
- **Line 1** is the moment you *enter* the situation.
- **Line 6** is when you are *leaving* it, on the verge of change.

In hexagram 23, the only yang line is precisely in **position 6**. It is Christ on the cross, at the moment of dying, just as the situation is ending. The yang line is on the exit edge.

For that yang line to be reborn as **line 1 of hexagram 24** (The Return), it must **completely exit the hexagram**, crossing a space where there is no linear representation. That space is hexagram 2: pure yin, the fertile void, Holy Saturday.

**Holy Saturday does not belong to linear time.** It is a timeless state, a "non-time." That is why there is no intermediate hexagram in the King Wen sequence: hexagram 2 represents that state outside time, the moment when the yang line has disappeared, is "outside the hexagram," traveling an invisible path to be reborn.

## The Hidden Core: The Nuclear Hexagram

But there is an even deeper detail, which reinforces this reading in an almost overwhelming way.

In the I Ching, there is the concept of the **nuclear hexagram** (or *hu gua*, "mutual hexagram"). It is formed by taking the inner lines of a hexagram:

- The **lower nuclear** is formed with lines 2, 3, and 4.
- The **upper nuclear** is formed with lines 3, 4, and 5.

The nuclear hexagram represents the **internal structure**, the hidden heart, the essence of the process described by a hexagram. It is what is at the center, what sustains from within.

Now:

- The nuclear hexagram of **23 (Bō)** is **hexagram 2 (Kūn)** .
- The nuclear hexagram of **24 (Fù)** is also **hexagram 2 (Kūn)** .

That is: **the same pure yin that symbolizes Holy Saturday is the internal core of both Friday and Sunday**.

Death and resurrection are not two separate things: **they share the same silent center**. Holy Saturday is not "between" Friday and Sunday: it is the **heart of both**. It is the matrix from which both collapse and rebirth emerge.

This has a profound theological implication: the promise of the resurrection points to the fact that death is not a definitive or final state, but a state of non-time. Time is the way we experience eternity in this plane, and hence there must necessarily be death and birth. Death is not the end: it is the threshold.

## The I Ching as a Notational Tool

In the video that inspired this article, I mentioned that "the I Ching reveals itself as a notational tool, a mathematical language, for thinking about these mysteries."

What does that mean?

In mathematics, a **notation system** is a finite set of symbols and rules designed to express concepts, objects, and operations precisely, concisely, and unambiguously.

For example, our positional decimal system (with Arabic numerals) makes arithmetic operations much easier than Roman numerals, because **the place a digit occupies tells us its magnitude** (units, tens, hundreds...). Position is meaning.

In the same way, the I Ching has its own **positional grammar**: six lines, each in a specific position, each able to be yin or yang. This structure allows thinking about change, time, and transformation with a clarity and economy of means that other symbolic forms do not achieve.

The I Ching is not just an oracle. It is a **representational system** that encodes situations, processes, and transformations in a binary structure of six positions. It is, in a sense, a formal language for change.

When we say the I Ching is a notational tool, we mean exactly that: its system of positions and lines gives us a structural language as powerful as a numbering system, but applied to the flow of situations, not quantities.

And it is precisely that notational power that allows us to see, with such clarity, the parallel between the Easter Triduum and hexagrams 23, 2, and 24. It is not a superficial coincidence: it is a **structural correspondence** that emerges from the system itself.

---

## Conclusion

The Easter Triduum and hexagrams 23, 2, and 24 of the I Ching offer us the same truth from two different languages:

- **Good Friday (23 Bō):** the collapse of the old, the death of what has completed its cycle.
- **Holy Saturday (2 Kūn):** the fertile void, the non-time, the matrix of all that is new.
- **Easter Sunday (24 Fù):** the return, the resurrection, the yang reborn from below.

The King Wen sequence does not include 2 between 23 and 24 because Holy Saturday does not belong to linear time. It is a timeless state. And the fact that the nuclear hexagram of both is the same 2 reveals to us that death and resurrection share the same silent heart.

The I Ching, with its grammar of six lines and positions, gives us a **notational tool** for thinking about these mysteries. Not to explain them - because mystery, by definition, cannot be explained - but to **think them with clarity**, to see their structure, to recognize that death and rebirth are not two separate things but two sides of the same transformative process.

Collapse, the fertile void, and the return. Three days, three hexagrams, a single mystery.
